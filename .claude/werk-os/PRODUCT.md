# WERK Product Strategy

## Purpose
This file defines the product direction for WERK.

Claude must use this file to make product, roadmap, feature, UX, monetization and go-to-market decisions.

## Core Mission
Build WERK into a serious, scalable and revenue-generating AI product that solves a painful real-world problem better than existing alternatives.

## Product Philosophy
WERK should not be built as a feature collection.

WERK should be built as a product users understand, trust, adopt and pay for.

Every feature must improve at least one of:

- Revenue
- Retention
- Activation
- Trust
- Differentiation
- User speed
- User productivity
- Strategic moat

If a feature does not support one of these outcomes, challenge it.

## Target User
Always clarify the exact user before building.

For every feature ask:

- Who is this for?
- What pain does it solve?
- How often does the pain occur?
- How expensive is the pain?
- What does the user currently do instead?
- Why would they switch?
- Why would they pay?

## Product Principles
Prefer:
- Simple onboarding
- Fast time-to-value
- Clear user flows
- Strong trust signals
- High reliability
- Practical automation
- Beautiful but functional design

Avoid:
- Vanity features
- Complex flows
- Unclear AI magic
- Over-engineered dashboards
- Features without business impact
- Building before validating

## AI Product Rules
AI must create clear value.

Do not add AI for decoration.

AI features must be:
- Useful
- Reliable
- Explainable enough
- Fast enough
- Safe enough
- Measurable

For every AI feature ask:
- What does AI do better than a normal workflow?
- What happens when AI is wrong?
- How does the user verify output?
- What data is needed?
- What privacy risk exists?
- What is the cost per usage?

## Monetization
WERK must be designed with monetization from the beginning.

Consider:
- Free trial
- Freemium
- Monthly subscription
- Usage-based pricing
- Team plans
- Enterprise plans
- Add-ons
- Premium AI usage
- White-label or partner models

Pricing must reflect value, not effort.

## Go-To-Market
Every product decision should consider distribution.

Ask:
- How will users find this?
- What is the strongest acquisition channel?
- What is the first niche?
- What proof is needed?
- What objection blocks conversion?
- What makes the product shareable?

## Roadmap Rules
Prioritize by:
1. User pain
2. Revenue potential
3. Speed to launch
4. Technical feasibility
5. Strategic differentiation
6. Retention impact
7. Risk reduction

Do not build advanced features before the core value proposition works.

## MVP Definition
The MVP is not the smallest product.

The MVP is the smallest version that proves:
- Someone has the problem
- WERK solves it better
- Users understand it
- Users return
- Users are willing to pay or strongly signal willingness to pay

## Metrics
Track metrics that prove real value.

Important metrics:
- Activation rate
- Time to first value
- Retention
- Weekly active users
- Conversion rate
- Churn
- Revenue
- Usage frequency
- Cost per user
- AI cost per task
- Support burden

Avoid vanity metrics unless they support decisions.

## Competitive Advantage
WERK must develop a moat.

Potential moats:
- Workflow depth
- Proprietary data
- Better UX
- Better AI orchestration
- Distribution
- Integrations
- Brand trust
- Speed of execution
- Community
- Enterprise readiness

Claude must challenge weak moat claims.

## Product Decision Output
For product decisions, respond with:

1. Executive product decision
2. Target user
3. User pain
4. Proposed solution
5. Why this matters
6. Revenue impact
7. UX implications
8. Technical implications
9. Risks
10. Recommended next step

## Final Rule
Do not optimize WERK for looking impressive.

Optimize WERK for solving a painful problem, getting adopted, retaining users and generating revenue.
